# Content Research - April 29, 2026

## 🔥 Trending Topics This Week

### 1. "Profound Autism" and High-Support-Needs Debate (HOT TOPIC)
A fresh federal autism policy discussion is centering on people with the highest support needs. STAT reported that the Interagency Autism Coordinating Committee met for the first time in 19 months and focused on proposals around high-support-needs autistic people, including changes to the scientific definition of "profound autism."

**Why it matters for SU:** Parent spaces are likely to split between two valid concerns: families needing clearer services for children with intensive support needs, and autistic advocates worried about segregation or stigmatizing labels.

**Content opportunity:** Lead with nuance, not debate bait.
- "High support needs vs. profound autism: what parents should understand"
- "Support labels should unlock help, not shrink a child's humanity"
- "How to ask for intensive supports without using deficit-only language"

### 2. Autism Self-Diagnosis and TikTok Misinformation Is Still Hot
MUSC's April 16 article keeps circulating around social media-driven autism self-diagnosis. The broader conversation: autism content helps people find language and community, but short-form videos can also blur signs, traits, diagnosis, and lived identity.

**Parent angle:** This is especially relevant for parents of tweens and teens who are learning about autism online.

**Content opportunity:**
- "Your teen says TikTok made them wonder if they're autistic: what to do next"
- "3 questions to ask before trusting an autism video"
- "Self-discovery is valid. A 30-second checklist is not a diagnosis."

### 3. Sensory Rooms and Sensory Accommodations Are Newsworthy Again
A Jamaica Gleaner story today covered a school breaking ground on a sensory room after a parent-teacher conference idea. Search results this week also surfaced sensory processing guides and IEP/school accommodation content.

**Why it matters:** Sensory content performs well because it is practical, visual, and highly shareable. The gap is moving from generic "sensory hacks" to specific school asks.

**Content opportunity:**
- "What to ask for if your school does not have a sensory room"
- "A sensory room is not a reward, it is access"
- "5 low-budget sensory accommodations schools can start tomorrow"

### 4. IEP Goals Are Shifting Toward Strength-Based Language
Search results surfaced IEP goal resources emphasizing that a well-written IEP goal should not only target deficits. Neurodiversity-affirming IEP conversations are growing, especially around ableist goals, parent rights, and sensory/communication accommodations.

**Content opportunity:**
- "Rewrite this ableist IEP goal with me"
- "The IEP phrase parents should question: age-appropriate behavior"
- "Ask for support in behavior, sensory, and communication, not just academics"

## 💡 Fresh Content Angles

### 1. "When the Label Debate Hits Your Family"
Use the profound autism discussion to create a calm explainer for parents who feel caught between advocacy camps.

**Carousel idea:**
1. The debate is not just semantics
2. Families need services that match real support needs
3. Autistic people deserve language that does not erase personhood
4. Better question: what support unlocks safety, communication, and dignity?
5. Script for your next meeting: "My child needs intensive support with..."

### 2. "The Sensory Room Backup Plan"
Most families do not have access to beautiful sensory rooms. Give parents practical substitutes for school and home.

**Post angles:**
- "No sensory room? Ask for these 6 alternatives"
- "A quiet corner can be an accommodation, not a privilege"
- "How to write sensory breaks into the IEP"

### 3. "Mental Health Month for Autism Parents"
May content should not be generic anxiety/depression posts. Tie it to autistic kids and caregivers.

**Post angles:**
- "Anxiety can look like refusal, rage, silence, or stomachaches"
- "Autistic burnout in kids is not laziness"
- "Parent burnout check: 5 signs you are running on fumes"
- "How to tell the difference between meltdown recovery and depression warning signs"

### 4. "Strength-Based IEP Rewrite Series"
Turn common deficit-based IEP phrases into neuroaffirming, measurable goals.

**Examples:**
- Instead of "will maintain eye contact," try "will use a preferred communication method to show attention or request clarification"
- Instead of "will sit still," try "will access movement tools while staying engaged in instruction"
- Instead of "will reduce disruptive behavior," try "will request a break or sensory support using AAC, gesture, card, or words"

### 5. "Teen Social Media Literacy for Autism Content"
Parents need a non-dismissive way to discuss online autism content with teens.

**Post angles:**
- "Do not shame the search. Guide it."
- "Green flags and red flags in autism content"
- "What to save, what to question, when to seek an evaluation"
